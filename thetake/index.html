TheTake

I have a passion for the future of multimedia experiences. In my current role, I create those experiences. I design and develop augmented video experiences that sit on top of existing network video solutions

Background

What we do

At TheTake, we serve two purposes: enable the TV/movie studio to monetize their content with highly relevant contextual ads and help the user to identify the product they see on screen. How this happens is highly dependent on your viewing device and the content owner. We run on set top boxes as an extra feature for on-demand movies, smart TVs, TV studio websites, entertainment/celebrity news sites, movie trailer sites, and more. Our experience identifying products and building a massive database of products and images has enabled us to create neural networks that can identify people, clothing, and other things - scene-by-scene - in an automated way.

Evolution of the tagged video UI

Our viewer interface has evolved a bit since the beginning. Usability and A/B testing, feedback from network execs/UX designers/lawyers, feedback from partner marketers, and more has shaped the product to what it is today.

The first iteration of our product overlaid dots on top of the products seen on screen; you could hover over them to see more details or click them to launch a modal to buy it or shop similar products in a lower price range. Obviously this approach could only be used on pause because it completely cluttered the screen, but what really caused us to move on was the legal concerns of the studios because this overlay might imply an actor was endorsing a product.

The next version dropped the dots in favor of a more removed listing of products. We wanted an interface that didn’t obscure the content and that could potentially run while the content is playing. We landed on a list of bubbles that expands/contracts on hover or pause.

Bubbles

This new interface had engagement issues. The data made it seem like most people accidentally expanded the bubble list and never touched it after that. The bubbles alone were very bare and provided no context as to what they were (unless one was of a product very prominent on screen while engaged), so we added guidance banners that disappeared upon hover. They also reappeared at select points in the video to attract attention to a look of the night - this in particular played well with one network’s audience for reality programming but did nothing for another network’s scripted shows.

Notification Bar

The data showed that a majority of users went full screen at some point during their sessions. An evaluation of the full screen experience brought to light that the bubbles could be potentially too diminutive on a larger screen and due to their contextual nature provided limited utility (only what’s on screen) so users would quickly forget it was even there. Because of this, we introduced a full screen experience for browsing all the outfits in the video at one time, while the video still played.

The Next Version

We got feedback from our partners and started getting requirements from potential new ones. This sparked a more serious redesign rather than our incremental, A/B testing approach for many of the previous changes described here. We did on-site user tests to get a feel for how people who were new to the product were using the interface. We synthesized this data into the following goals:

  • Less like an ad, more like a feature
  • Integrate seamlessly into existing UIs
  • Key metrics: drive clicks vs views

As I mentioned, the full screen experience was our latest redesign. The user testing showed that people had issues with the placement of the button that launches the experience: too small of a target, moves away if you overshoot it with the mouse. We tried many designs and settled on one that was less problematic, but also unfortunately made it seem like the button was a label for the expanding bubbles.

Full Screen Shopping Button

Further iterations on this full screen experience included: inserting our own custom icon into the studio playbar that launches the experience (integrating better with their UI too), launching the experience automatically during the credits and also when you pause in full screen, and autoscrolling the experience to the top outfit currently on screen when it is launched. The icon got practically no engagement. Auto launching the experience saw an uptick in engagement but not a lot; it seems that this A/B test audience is highly resistant to engaging with features outside of the primary task at hand. Autoscrolling yielded no noticeable gains.

Custom Playbar Icon

Icon Options

All our testing and product telemetry suggested that our biggest issue was getting people to engage with the product in the first place. People who used it and understood what it was had really high engagement. Users reported initially reading the overlay as an ad, so they ignored it entirely. To achieve the goal of less like an ad, more like a feature we started reimagining our interface as something that is a seamless part of the player’s interface/chrome.

We also started getting more partners that wanted to integrate us into their set-top boxes/smart TVs/TV apps, meaning we needed an interface that worked well for the 10 foot experience. They also requested that additional metadata be shown alongside the product data (like music, locations, actor info, etc.). Our primary goal was still identifying the products you see and showing you where to get them, but adding this other functionality allowed us to better position it as a feature to the end-user. Furthermore, these changes would allow us to bring our interface in line with the typical video player interface seen on TV-attached devices today, hopefully reducing the barrier to teaching users what our stuff is and how to use it.

Playbar Integration

Afterword

We are still testing iterations on the new playbar design and A/B testing against the control (our old expanding bubbles look). Engagement is actually higher with the new design - for those who use the feature at all. The click through rate, overall, is about the same for both designs. Since product views are counted differently (hover for old design, click for new design) there is now more intent behind a product view - which explains the fact that the new design converts more product views into buy clicks (user clicks through to a merchant site to buy the product). Both views and buys can be monetized, so it’s important to measure both; obviously, a buy has more purchase intent so it is more valuable to merchants.

The real problem is that less people are engaging with our product with the new design. No great insight has been revealed as to why that is, but we are in the process of testing some designs focused around engagement. When tagged products first appear on screen, we have in the past (with good success) used special intro animations to call attention to our features; likewise, we have used the look of the night to grab attention later in the video. Here we try to bring those to this new design. Quick and dirty mockups of animation were made in PowerPoint.

The end of the episode presents an opportunity to capture users once they have finished their primary goal (watching the video). End cards are used elsewhere in the video player industry to surface additional/related video, ads, etc. We are creating some experiments to test this here - against the control (old design) which automatically pops up the full screen experience in the credits.

End Card

Finally, a design to tweak the way products are surfaced to the user in the playbar is primed for testing after these other tests are complete. Since the bubbles don’t change while the playbar is open/visible, the user doesn’t know that there is more data to explore at that time - but we could notify them that new outfits are available to be seen. This helps capture users who are dismissing the interface as unimportant because of its static look.
